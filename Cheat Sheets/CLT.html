Central Limit Theorem¶

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that when you take sufficiently large samples from any population, the distribution of the sample means will approximate a normal (bell-shaped) distribution, regardless of the population’s original distribution. This is one of the most powerful results in probability and statistics because it allows us to use normal distribution methods even when the underlying data is not normal.

Key Points¶

Conditions:

  • Random samples must be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.).
  • The population must have a finite variance.
  • Sample size should be sufficiently large (commonly n>30 is considered adequate).

Result:

  • The mean of the sampling distribution equals the population $mean (\mu ).$
  • The standard deviation of the sampling distribution (standard error) is $\sigma /\sqrt{n}$, where $ \sigma$ is the population standard deviation.

How CLT Works¶

  1. Start with any population
    • The population can be skewed, uniform, or irregular.
    • As long as it has a finite mean $(\mu )$ and variance $(\sigma ^2)$, CLT applies.
  2. Take random samples
    • Draw repeated samples of size n from the population.
    • Each sample will have its own mean $\bar {X}_n.$
  3. Build the sampling distribution
    • Collect all those sample means.
    • Plot them as a histogram → this is the sampling distribution of the mean.
  4. Observe the shape
    • As n increases, the distribution of \bar {X}_n becomes approximately normal.
    • Even if the original population is not normal, the sample means trend toward a bell curve.
  5. Standardize the mean
    • The standardized form is:
    $ Z=\frac{\bar {X}_n-\mu }{\sigma /\sqrt{n}} $
    • As $n\rightarrow \infty$ , Z follows a standard normal distribution N(0,1).

Intuition

  • Small samples: The sample means may still reflect the skewness of the population.
  • Large samples: The “law of averages” kicks in, smoothing irregularities.
  • Result: The distribution of sample means stabilizes around the true mean, with variability shrinking as n grows.

Example: Rolling a Die¶

Step 1: Define the population

  • Population: outcomes of rolling a fair six-sided die → values {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.
  • Population mean:

$ \mu =\frac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6}=3.5 $

  • Population variance:

$ \sigma ^2=\frac{(1-3.5)^2+\dots +(6-3.5)^2}{6}=2.92 $

  • Population standard deviation: $\sigma \approx 1.71$.

Notice: the population distribution is uniform, not normal.

Step 2: Take samples

  • Suppose we take random samples of size n=30.
  • Each sample has 30 die rolls, and we compute the sample mean $\bar {X}_{30}.$

Step 3: Sampling distribution

  • Repeat this process many times (say 10,000 samples).
  • Collect all the sample means.
  • Plot them → the histogram looks approximately normal, centered at 3.5.

Step 4: Standardize

The standardized sample mean is: $ Z=\frac{\bar {X}_{30}-3.5}{1.71/\sqrt{30}} $ As n grows, Z follows N(0,1).

Step 5: Interpretation

  • Even though the die rolls are uniform, the distribution of sample means is normal.
  • The spread shrinks as n increases, because the standard error is $\sigma /\sqrt{n}.$
  • This allows us to use normal-based inference (z-scores, confidence intervals) for dice rolls, coin flips, or any finite-variance population.

Key Applications of CLT¶

  1. Economics & Social Sciences
    • Surveys & Polls: CLT justifies using sample means to estimate population parameters (e.g., average income, voting preferences).
    • Policy Analysis: Economists rely on CLT to generalize from sample data to entire populations.
  2. Finance
    • Stock Market Returns: Daily returns are not normally distributed, but averages over time approximate normality.
    • Risk Management: CLT underpins Value-at-Risk (VaR) models and portfolio analysis.
  3. Quality Control & Manufacturing
    • Process Monitoring: Sample averages of product measurements (e.g., weight, length) follow normal distribution, enabling control charts.
    • Defect Analysis: CLT helps predict defect rates and set tolerance limits.
  4. Biology & Medicine
    • Clinical Trials: Patient outcomes averaged across groups approximate normality, allowing valid statistical tests.
    • Genetics: Traits influenced by many small factors (polygenic traits) tend toward normal distribution.
  5. Engineering & Physical Sciences
    • Signal Processing: Noise from multiple independent sources tends toward normal distribution.
    • Reliability Testing: CLT helps estimate average lifetimes of components.

Limitations & Risks¶

  • Small samples: CLT may not hold; distributions can remain skewed.
  • Infinite variance distributions (e.g., Cauchy) break CLT assumptions.
  • Dependence: CLT requires independent samples; correlated data (like time series) may need adjustments.

Practical Takeaway¶

CLT is the backbone of inferential statistics. It allows us to:

  • Use z-tests, t-tests, and confidence intervals reliably.
  • Generalize from sample data to populations.
  • Apply normal distribution tools across diverse fields, from polling to engineering.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Step 1: Define the population
# Let's use a uniform distribution (not normal) to prove CLT works
population = np.random.uniform(low=0, high=10, size=100000)

# Step 2: Sampling function
def sample_means(sample_size, num_samples):
    means = []
    for _ in range(num_samples):
        sample = np.random.choice(population, size=sample_size, replace=True)
        means.append(np.mean(sample))
    return means

# Step 3: Generate sample means for different sample sizes
means_n5 = sample_means(sample_size=5, num_samples=10000)
means_n30 = sample_means(sample_size=30, num_samples=10000)
means_n100 = sample_means(sample_size=100, num_samples=10000)

# Step 4: Plot histograms
plt.figure(figsize=(15, 4))

plt.subplot(1, 3, 1)
plt.hist(means_n5, bins=30, color='skyblue', edgecolor='black')
plt.title("Sample Size = 5")

plt.subplot(1, 3, 2)
plt.hist(means_n30, bins=30, color='lightgreen', edgecolor='black')
plt.title("Sample Size = 30")

plt.subplot(1, 3, 3)
plt.hist(means_n100, bins=30, color='salmon', edgecolor='black')
plt.title("Sample Size = 100")

plt.suptitle("Central Limit Theorem in Action", fontsize=16)
plt.show()
